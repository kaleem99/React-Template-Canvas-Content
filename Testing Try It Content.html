1. Introduction

1. Introduction

Learning outcomes: 

LO1: Discuss the sustainability-related risks and opportunities that businesses are currently facing and the commercial imperative for sustainability.

LO2: Show how businesses can create shared value through their operations and supply chain.

LO3: Illustrate how sustainability initiatives act as a driver for innovation and competitive advantage.

Businesses are increasingly becoming aware of the risks that sustainability challenges pose to their operations, along with the opportunities for long-term resilience presented by addressing these risks.

These challenges are not only numerous, but interconnected: economic, societal, geopolitical, technological, and environmental. Many are largely a consequence of the systems pressures and trends discussed in Module 1.

Not only do these sustainability challenges impact the current operating context for business, but – given the increasing scale of the challenges and the level of uncertainty and risk they present – they also necessitate a radical rethink of traditional business and operating models. This innovation becomes crucial as decision-making becomes increasingly difficult and decision-making based on prior experience becomes less reliable (McKinsey Global Institute, 2020).

The opportunities for businesses in addressing these challenges – both at present and in the future – are in some cases becoming better understood and accepted. For example, some of these opportunities include: 

  • Interconnectedness between business and society: This describes the increasing understanding of the interconnectedness between a business and the society in which it operates, in addition to the need for a business to make a positive social contribution in order to maintain its social license to operate. This is also where the concept of shared value becomes important.

  • Surveillance of corporate practice: The describes the need for corporate transparency through mechanisms such as increasing reporting requirements, social media and civil society activism. These increased levels of transparency require businesses to operate more responsibly.

  • Manage and mitigate interruptions: This describes the need to better manage and mitigate any interruptions to operations and the production of deliverables at a reliable, consistent and predictable rate. This is especially true for businesses that operate on a global scale and have – for example – raw materials, suppliers, manufacturing sites, office headquarters and customers across the globe.

  • Reduce costs: This describes the opportunities to reduce costs – for example through energy efficiency – and to possibly create new revenue streams for existing businesses. For example, Google also produces and sells clean energy.

  • Growth and maturation: This describes the growth and maturation of new sectors, industries and technologies. For example, the renewable energy industry where almost every source of clean energy can compete with the costs of energy from fossil fuel sources and the opportunities that the sharing economy, the circular economy and the platform business model services economy provide. For example, platforms that facilitate asset sharing or the exchange of goods and services (Frankfurt School of Finance & Management, 2019).

This set of notes provides an overview of some of the major risks businesses currently face and will continue to face in the near future, as well as the commercial opportunities that dealing with some of the significant sustainability-related challenges present. You will also learn about the commercial imperative for embracing sustainability and making it part of your overall business strategy, as well as the concept of shared value creation. Lastly, the link between sustainability and innovation and their contribution to competitive advantage is explored.

2. Business risks and opportunities

2. Business risks and opportunities

Businesses are becoming acutely aware of the inherent risks of doing business on a global scale. Their dependence on diverse natural resources for raw materials, an international customer base, and a heavy reliance on information and communication technologies all need to be carefully managed if plans for growth are not to be undermined. Additionally, companies need to grasp the new commercial opportunities that addressing sustainability challenges present and need to be agile enough to adapt their business models accordingly to accommodate the global shifts ahead.

2.1 Risks

The risk landscape is constantly changing on both a local level and a regional level. Given the interconnectedness of the global economy and the world we live in, risks can also be global. While these risks have the potential to negatively impact businesses regardless of location, some countries are more prone to certain risks than others. For example, businesses in arid regions face a higher risk of being affected by potential water shortages due to climate change.

2.1.1 World Economic Forum Global Risks Report

Each year, as part of its Global Risks Initiative, the World Economic Forum (WEF) compiles an annual Global Risks Report. The purpose of the report is to understand the major risks the world faces. Each report is based on a survey conducted with experts and decision-makers in multi-stakeholder communities, and with the WEF’s Global Shapers Community: a “network of young people driving dialogue, action and change” (World Economic Forum, 2020).

The report survey asks respondents to consider a set of global risks (societal, technological, economic, environmental or geopolitical) over a 10-year time horizon, and to rate each risk according to their perceived likelihood of it occurring and the resulting impact if it were to occur. Climate change and related environmental issues – such as biodiversity loss and extreme weather events – make up the top five risks in terms of likelihood in the 2020 report (16th edition). They also make up five of the top 10 risks in terms of impact. Societal risks are also highlighted as a growing area of fundamental concern (as outlined in Figure IV of the 2021 report) (World Economic Forum, 2021). To view the likelihood of these risks alongside their potential impact download the 2021 report and view figure 2 on page 3. 

The report also examines the interconnections among risks, which can help leaders to prioritise areas for action and plan for contingencies (World Economic Forum, 2020). Importantly, climate change is a risk multiplier (DNV GL Sustainia and the United Nations Global Compact, 2019); this means that it amplifies and aggravates other risks that negatively impact the achievement of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). For example, the risk of an increasingly destabilising society could negatively impact food and water supply, thereby increasing competition over natural resources and a loss of livelihoods (UNEP, n.d.).

2.1.2 Trade-offs

Overcoming the risk landscape by transitioning to a low-carbon economy does not come without its own risks. Sometimes, trade-offs between conflicting priorities in risk management must take place. A trade-off involves “a balancing of factors all of which are not available at the same time” (Merriam-Webster, 2020). 

These risks include the possibility of stranded assets and dealing with challenges that may include technological readiness, upscaling challenges and institutional barriers; for example, a delay in the implementation of policies that support action against climate change (IPCC, 2019). Risks also include the possibility of exacerbating socio-economic inequalities, even if only in the short- to medium-term: 

“…profound issues around the future job market, health and safety, and the broader fate of communities. For both investors and workers, transitioning quickly, effectively and equitably will be this generation’s challenge.” 

(World Economic Forum, 2020)

2.2 Opportunities

Despite the risks businesses face, the changing landscape simultaneously presents many commercial opportunities for businesses. Businesses are increasingly being incentivised to explore and seize these opportunities by changing investor focus and funding requirements (for example, through the United Nations Principles for Responsible Investment), changing market prices (for example, the prices of carbon and renewable energy), gaining an increasing understanding of the limit to natural resources, developing new regulations, and changing consumer and employee preferences (DNV GL Sustainia and the United Nations Global Compact, 2019).

These opportunities include the development and maturity of new technologies, products and services, and the emergence of multi-stakeholder partnerships. Creativity, a high-risk tolerance, and an entrepreneurial mindset that embraces change and collaboration will benefit businesses as they seek to navigate the uncertain global business terrain. The sections that follow highlight a few examples of new economic opportunities that respond to these risks. 

Note that the opportunities associated with the changing landscape must be assessed against having consideration for the limits to economic growth that exist given the limited resources of the planet that growth is reliant on (Raworth, 2018).

2.2.1 Growth in new goods and services

“The market for climate solutions is huge and difficult to measure by exact numbers”, but clear trends to reduce emissions and improve climate resilience have created major opportunities that growing numbers of businesses are capitalising on (DNV GL Sustainia and the United Nations Global Compact, 2019). 

Low-carbon goods and services are not a specific category of product, but rather span many traditional sectors, thereby providing opportunities for many businesses to take advantage of market growth. Sectors that are strongly affected by climate change and specifically hold opportunities in this regard include connectivity, energy, finance, the built environment, mobility and food. “The estimates of the total market value of the six sectors exceeds trillions of dollars – economic opportunities that can be realised over a relatively short number of years” (DNV GL Sustainia and the United Nations Global Compact, 2019). 

For example, connectivity providers have an opportunity to contribute to climate mitigation and adaptation solutions through their services and their access to data, in addition to a drive to a more inclusive society. This can be done by enabling distributed electricity transmission, sharing vital information (in terms of the weather) to remote farming communities, helping the unbanked to access finance, and helping to increase access to education. There are also numerous opportunities for the sector to reduce its energy consumption and to contribute less to e-waste and over-consumption (DNV GL Sustainia and the United Nations Global Compact, 2019).

2.2.2 Frugal innovation

As resources become more costly and constrained, frugal innovation is being developed in all stages of the value chain. Companies are introducing new products and services – often with greater economic and social value – at a lower cost using fewer natural and financial resources. The goal is not to create cheap offerings, but rather more effective offerings in terms of affordability, simplicity, quality and sustainability. Frugal innovation is a new mindset; it is a flexible approach that perceives resource constraints as a growth opportunity rather than a debilitating challenge. It is closely connected to the circular economy.

An example of frugal innovation would be Unilever’s approach of reformulating existing products to require fewer resources during production and to use, and generate, less waste. GlaxoSmithKline provides another example in its move to invest in many smaller research and development projects that can either be expanded or closed down dependant on the efficacy and viability of their research results. Additionally, M-KOPA’s “home solar solution in a box” provides those without access to the electricity grid in Kenya with a rooftop solar panel that provides lighting, a solar radio and a cell phone charger (Radjou, 2017; Radjou & Prabhu, 2014; Radjou & Prabhu, 2013). 

Partnering with external stakeholders in new ways is another example; this gives businesses access to external resources and knowledge that enable innovation. Examples include crowdsourcing frugal solutions, as was the case with the General Electric Company (GE) when it was developing next generation aircraft brackets (Radjou & Prabhu, 2014). The way businesses engage with external stakeholders to debate and reflect on external perspectives on the business’s way of doing things serves as another example. This can be a catalyst for new, more effective ways of operating within a business (Stark, 2009; Etzion & Ferraro, 2015; De Villiers, et al., 2014; Whelan & Fink, 2016). This example of external stakeholder engagement signifies the increased need for businesses to collaborate rather than compete when it comes to resilience.

2.2.3 Improved resilience

“Increasingly, we see evidence and acknowledgement that climate change presents financial risk to the global economy. According to the United Nations, delays in tackling this issue could cost companies nearly US $1.2 trillion over the next 15 years” (TCFD, 2019). 

The physical effects of sustainability challenges like climate change will be disruptive; businesses must act now to be more resilient to these risks. Acting now means doing the work now to understand businesses’ impacts of operating so that they can better understand and mitigate the risks they face, and navigate the unexpected (Whelan & Fink, 2016). 

For example, understanding the sustainability impacts of: 

  • A supply chain: Enables greater supply chain resilience.

  • A business or project you invest in (via capital or loans): Helps protect the financial value of the investment.

3. The commercial imperative for sustainability

3. The commercial imperative for sustainability

By addressing risks and seizing new commercial opportunities, conducting business sustainably has the potential to benefit a business’s bottom line and to build in long-term resilience. This includes future access to funding and insurance (Bonini & Swartz, 2014; McKinsey Global Institute, 2020).

For example, financial institutions that address sustainability by investing in the circular economy early create potential value for themselves by obtaining a “first mover” advantage (in other words, a competitive advantage by being early to market with this product or service) and allocating capital to areas of future growth with less risk.

4. Sustainable business models

4. Sustainable business models

“A company cannot achieve long-term profits without embracing purpose and considering the needs of a broad range of stakeholders” 

(Fink, 2021).

A business model can be thought of as a plan that describes a proposed strategy, or practise, for a company’s success. This strategy usually consists of value creation, value delivery, and value capture (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010). These models delve into how a business might create shared value, however, many traditional business models are more interested in creating value for the customer, and make no reference to sustainability. 

The traditional three steps to creating a business model can be broken down as follows:


  1. Value creation: This is the creation of value by creating a product, or service, that costs less to create than the customer is willing to pay for it.

  2. Value delivery: With the use of marketing and branding, customers may be willing to buy into your product when they believe in what you are selling. Here, you can create value through what you are promising to deliver. 

  3. Value capture: You may wish to give your customers more value for money than your competitors do with similar products. In the long-run, customers will feel as if they are getting more, and are more likely to trust the product.

(Myler, 2013)

Sustainable business models focus more on the social, environmental, and economic value created, also known as the 3Ps: people, planet, profit (Kraaijenbrink, 2019). In a sustainable business plan, a company should gauge the beneficial and detrimental impact they have on people, such as their employees, customers, the communities in which they operate, and the people they do business with. In terms of the planet, businesses are asked to measure their use, and waste, of nature resources. And, for the last P, profit, a sustainable business should not only measure their own financial gain, but should look at the larger economic impact they have created, and what they hope to bring to the community economy, through job creation, and the like (Kraaijenbrink, 2019). 

The Sustainable Business Models are: 


  • Personalisation 

  • Closed loop

  • Asset sharing

  • Usage-based pricing

  • Collaborative ecosystem 

  • Agility

The six business model shifts happening in response to value are: 

  1. The platform shift: Instead of only valuing the product, or transaction, value is also created in the interaction. This shift links people who are looking for something with those who already have it. Think of examples like AirBnB which connects people who have extra residential space, with those who are looking to rent it.

  2. The digital shift: More and more value is given to digital platforms and what they can offer, including the value gained from tracking consumer engagement and bettering customer experience.

  3. The services shift: Value is not always about a physical product, but about the service benefit your product gives the customer. For example, UberEats is not just about selling food, but about getting that food to the customer, the benefit being that the customer does not need to leave their home.

  4. The exponential shift: Exponential growth is emphasised through global change, by helping a greater cause, businesses can gain the attention of more investors and gain other upscaling opportunities.

  5. The stakeholder shift: This shift aims to make customers, partners, and producers, of the product, or service, content in order to boost the business as a whole.

  6. The circular shift: Instead of disposing of products once they have been used, products are reused to save and create value.

(Business Model Inc Blog, 2021a-f)

The Cambridge Value Mapping Tool is a guided step-by-step process that was conceived to help companies create value propositions to support sustainable business modelling (Bocken et al, 2013). The tool gives practitioners a new way to gain a deeper understanding of value and create new economic, social, and environmental benefits for their business.

Creating shared value is an example of an approach that seeks to connect social progress and business value. The purpose of shared value is for companies to find a way to turn social problems into business opportunities that help to solve these problems instead (Shared Value Initiative, 2016). Furthermore, it is about creating value for both the business (in the form of profits) and society (for example, through job creation, training and development initiatives). Shared value is not simply about “doing the right thing” by donating money to causes. It is a management strategy that aims to empower individuals and communities while simultaneously delivering on financial value for a business (Driffill, Hacking, & Styles, 2016).

5. The link between sustainability and innovation

5.1 A brief introduction to innovation

Innovation is the formulation – or invention – of new products and services with the intent to introduce them into the marketplace (Hacking, Driffill, Dee, & Friedman, 2015). Innovation can be applied in numerous different contexts; this includes technological innovation and product innovation, process innovation, and policy and social innovation (Driffill, Hacking, & Stiles, 2016). 

Although innovation is explored in more detail in Module 5, it is important to note the link between companies that pursue sustainability, and innovation (Aronson, 2013). According to a report by Joel Makower, companies who lead in the sustainability field are 400 percent more likely to be considered leaders in innovation (Makower, 2013). Moreover, it is also believed that a strong and positive correlation between innovation performance and financial performance exists (Whelan & Fink, 2016; McKinsey Quarterly, 2019).

Innovation in sustainability can be categorised into three levels, namely incremental, radical and fundamental innovation, and entails the following:  

  1. Incremental innovation: This involves step-by step improvement in business practices, products and services. Such innovation is widely practiced to reduce losses in health, safety, environment, quality, energy, materials, as well as to lower costs and improve efficiency. Incremental innovation has the advantages of being relatively fast, low cost, and low risk. It has the disadvantage of creating only incremental change, and is not capable of driving transformative change. This is the dominant innovation mechanism in business today. 

  2. Radical innovation: This includes activities which result in dramatic change to practices, products, or services—typically incorporating new design thinking. The risks and investments required for radical innovation are significantly higher than for incremental innovation. Such innovation has higher uncertainty with regard to the technical aspects of the new design, the market demand, and the organisation’s ability to implement the necessary changes. However, this mechanism offers the potential for breakthrough change, and can dramatically enhance competitive advantage.

  3. Fundamental innovation: This is associated with the application of new scientific knowledge, or changes in societal values, to create a new mindset or paradigm. Fundamental innovation is highly disruptive, and will result in the reshaping of industries and markets.

(Diehl, Crul & Ryan 2009)

Ritala et al (2018), studied the business model innovation activities of companies in the S&P 500 Index and identified 9 archetypes of business model innovation. 5.2 Creating a competitive advantage through innovation

Innovation will be the only sustainable competitive advantage 21st century organisations will be able to attain (Cheam, 2015). This is based on the premise that today’s knowledge economy already grants businesses access to vast amounts of information, and that the difference between the best organisations and mediocre organisations will be their ability to use the information they are given to innovate and stay ahead of the curve. In order to reap the benefits of innovation, however, businesses must be prepared to take risks and incur short-term costs in the medium- to long-term. This requires commitment from top management and a willingness to persist despite the challenges.
